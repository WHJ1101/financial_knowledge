# Deep Research Workflow

Use this file when the user asks for current opportunities, ranked candidates, "最值得研究", "深度调研", or a full theme scan.

## Minimum Completion Standard

For a current theme scan, aim to complete these checks before the final answer:

- cover at least three value-chain layers;
- rank scarce layers before companies;
- inspect 25+ sources when tools and time allow;
- build a starting universe of 20+ candidates when the market is broad enough;
- cover obvious leaders, upstream suppliers, equipment, materials, testing, infrastructure, funds/ETFs, and adjacent beneficiaries;
- select the final 3-7 research priorities;
- explain what each final candidate constrains or sits closest to;
- support each top candidate with concrete evidence;
- state what could make the judgment wrong;
- downgrade at least one popular or obvious area when evidence or scarcity is weaker;
- give next checks the user can actually run.

If tools, source access, or time prevent that standard, label the answer as an initial pass and provide the exact verification path.

## Workflow

### 1. Scope The Request

Infer the missing pieces when reasonable:

- market: A股、港股、美股、台股、日本、韩国、欧洲、全球、私有公司图谱;
- theme: AI infrastructure, semiconductors, CPO, advanced packaging, robotics, power, cooling, materials, equipment, healthcare manufacturing, defense electronics, or the user's topic;
- time window: use 3-12 months for "now" unless the user says otherwise;
- output: priority layers, candidate rankings, evidence, risks, and next checks.

Ask only when the missing scope would materially change the answer.

### 2. Convert Theme To System Change

Write the practical chain:

`demand wave -> system pressure -> required technical change -> constrained layer`

Examples:

- AI clusters -> bandwidth and power pressure -> optical interconnect and switching upgrades -> lasers, DSP/ASICs, testing, packaging, substrates.
- AI servers -> power density and uptime pressure -> power conversion, transformers, switchgear, liquid cooling -> qualified equipment and components.
- Humanoid robotics -> actuator and sensing density -> precision reducers, motors, encoders, tactile sensing, batteries -> manufacturing yield and supplier qualification.

### 3. Build The Value-Chain Map

Use these layers as a checklist:

1. End customers and capex source.
2. System integrators and OEMs.
3. Modules and subsystems.
4. Chips, devices, and critical components.
5. Process, assembly, packaging, and testing.
6. Equipment and metrology.
7. Materials, consumables, and specialty inputs.
8. Physical infrastructure.

Keep buckets granular. For AI半导体, separate compute chips, EDA/IP, memory/storage, equipment, materials, testing, advanced packaging, optical links, PCB/CCL, power, and cooling.

### 4. Search For Scarce Layers

A scarce layer becomes interesting when several signals stack:

- customers cannot scale without it;
- supplier count is low;
- qualification is slow;
- expansion requires specialized equipment, permits, know-how, or material purity;
- customers show urgency through prepayments, capacity reservations, long-term contracts, expedited orders, or price acceptance;
- the market still classifies the company by an older business category.

After this step, write the layer ranking before moving to the company list.

Chinese opening pattern:

```text
先排产业链层级：设备平台、关键工艺设备、先进封装材料、国产算力芯片、普通零部件。
前几层更接近扩产和工艺升级的硬约束，后面几层需要更强订单和财务证据才能往前排。
```

### 5. Build The Candidate Universe

Avoid starting from the hottest tickers. Include names across:

- obvious leaders;
- chips, accelerators, EDA/IP, memory, storage, interconnect;
- upstream equipment and process-specific tools;
- materials, consumables, and specialty gases/chemicals;
- testing, metrology, advanced packaging, OSAT;
- PCB, CCL, optical links, server infrastructure;
- power, cooling, grid, data-center physical infrastructure;
- funds and ETFs when the user asks for vehicle exposure;
- lower-priority popular names that deserve explicit downgrading.

Classify each candidate plainly:

- controls the scarce layer;
- supplies the scarce layer;
- benefits from demand but has limited control;
- has exposure with weak pricing power;
- has a good story with weak proof.

### 6. Gather Current Evidence

Prioritize:

- filings, exchange disclosures, annual reports, quarterly reports, and announcements;
- investor relations materials, earnings transcripts, and presentations;
- official customer, order, tender, project, approval, and regulatory records;
- patents, standards, technical papers, trade publications, and industry associations;
- reputable financial media and specialist analysis;
- social posts only as lead generation.

For deep current scans, aim for a mix of 10+ filings/announcements, 5+ company or technical sources, and 5+ credible media/trade/association/project records.

### 7. Rank Candidates

Rank by:

- demand pressure;
- tightness of the scarce layer;
- supplier concentration;
- expansion difficulty;
- evidence strength;
- valuation gap or market misunderstanding;
- near-term events that could change investor perception;
- financing, governance, liquidity, accounting, geopolitical, and substitution risk.

Keep two rankings distinct:

1. **Layer ranking**: which parts of the system deserve attention first.
2. **Company ranking**: which companies best represent those layers with evidence.

For each final company, answer:

- What exactly does it constrain?
- Where does it sit in the chain?
- Why does it rank here?
- What evidence supports that rank?
- What would make the rank weaker?

### 8. Explain The Answer

Start with the conclusion:

- layers worth prioritizing;
- top names, funds, or ETFs to research first;
- why they rank higher;
- strongest proof and missing proof;
- popular areas that rank lower;
- main ways the view can be wrong;
- next checks.

Prefer normal prose. Add a compact table only for ranking or evidence comparison.
